\chapter{Hypothesis Testing: Chance or Real Effect?}
\label{ch:hypothesis}

Life is full of situations where we must decide whether an observed
result reflects a real effect or simply random chance. Is that coin
you are flipping really unfair, or did it just happen to land heads
several times in a row? Did a new medicine help patients recover, or
would they have improved anyway? Is one website design better than
another, or did it get lucky in a single experiment? In the previous
two chapters, we learned how probability describes uncertainty and how
randomness behaves when we collect data. Now we can use those ideas to
answer a new question: when should we attribute an observed result to
chance, and when should we view it as evidence of a real effect? In
this chapter, we learn how statisticians answer questions like these
using hypothesis testing. Rather than trying to prove something with
absolute certainty, hypothesis testing weighs the evidence in the data
against a baseline explanation and asks whether the observed result
would be unusual if only chance were operating. Along the way, we will
introduce null and alternative hypotheses, explore p-values and
permutation tests through simulation, study the possibility of making
incorrect decisions, and examine what happens when many hypotheses
are tested at the same time.


\section{Basic Concepts}

Every hypothesis test begins with two competing explanations. The
\emph{null hypothesis}\index{\emph{null hypothesis}}, written $H_0$,
is the baseline explanation. It often says that there is no real
difference, no effect, or no pattern beyond random variation. The
\emph{alternative hypothesis}\index{\emph{alternative hypothesis}},
written $H_1$, is the explanation we consider when the data look too
surprising under the null.

For example, suppose researchers are studying a new drug. The null
hypothesis might say that the drug has no effect compared with a
placebo. The alternative hypothesis might say that the drug does have
an effect. In an A-B test for a website, the null might say that two
page designs lead to the same sales rate, while the alternative says
that one design changes the sales rate.

The same setup appears in many other settings. Scientists might ask
whether the observed increase in global temperature could be explained
without human activity. A hospital might ask whether its 30-day
readmission rate is higher for one group of patients than for another.
A school might ask whether a new tutoring program improved test scores.
The details change, but the structure is the same: start with a
baseline explanation, then ask whether the data are too surprising for
that explanation.

The logic of a hypothesis test is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Assume the null hypothesis is true.
\item Work out what kinds of data would be common under that assumption.
\item Compare the observed data with those possibilities.
\item If the observed data are very unusual under $H_0$, reject $H_0$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Assume the Baseline, Then Check the Data}

Hypothesis testing is a little like proof by contradiction
\citep{reeves1980hypothesis}. In a mathematical proof by
contradiction, we start by assuming the opposite of what we want to
show. If that assumption leads to an impossibility, then the original
statement must be true.

In hypothesis testing, we also start with an assumption: the null
hypothesis. Then we ask what kinds of data we would expect if that
assumption were true. If the observed data are very far from what the
null hypothesis predicts, we reject $H_0$.

The difference is that statistics does not give the certainty of a
mathematical proof. Random events can produce surprising results. A
fair coin can land heads 8 times in 10 flips. A useless drug can look
helpful in one small experiment. So rejecting $H_0$ does not prove
$H_1$ beyond all doubt. It means that the observed data are unusual
enough under $H_0$ that we decide to reject the baseline explanation.
Similarly, failing to reject $H_0$ does not prove that $H_0$ is true.
It only means that the data did not give enough evidence against it.

As a warm-up, imagine flipping a fair coin 20 times and seeing 16
heads. One way to judge this result is to build a pretend world where
the coin is fair. We could simulate many sets of 20 fair-coin flips and
count how often 16 or more heads appear. The Lady Tasting Tea example
uses the same logic, but with cups instead of coins and with an exact
count of all possible arrangements.

\subsection{Type~I and Type~II Errors}

Because data are random, hypothesis tests can make mistakes. A
\emph{Type~I error}\index{\emph{Type~I error}} occurs when we reject
the null hypothesis even though it is true. This is also called a false
positive. For example, we might conclude that a drug works when it
does not.

The probability of making a Type~I error is controlled by the
\emph{significance level}\index{\emph{significance level}}, denoted by
$\alpha$. A common choice is $\alpha = 0.05$, which means that if
$H_0$ is true, the test is designed to reject it no more than 5\% of
the time.

A \emph{Type~II error}\index{\emph{Type~II error}} occurs when we fail
to reject $H_0$ even though there really is an effect. This is also
called a false negative. If the probability of a Type~II error is
$\beta$, then $1 - \beta$ is called the \emph{power}\index{\emph{power}}
of the test. Power measures how well the test detects a real effect.


\begin{example}[Lady tasting tea]
R. A. Fisher's ``Lady Tasting Tea'' experiment, described in his 1935 
book \emph{The Design of Experiments} \citep{fisher1935design},
became one of the earliest and 
most elegant demonstrations of formal hypothesis testing 
\citep{salsburg2002lady}. Fisher recounted an event at Rothamsted 
Experimental Station, where a colleague claimed she could tell by 
taste whether milk or tea had been poured first into a cup. To test 
this claim, Fisher designed an experiment involving eight cups of tea: 
four prepared with milk poured first, and four with tea poured first. 
The cups were presented in random order, and the lady was told that 
there were four of each kind. Her task was to identify the four cups 
with milk poured first. Assuming the null hypothesis, $H_0$, that she 
was guessing, Fisher calculated the probability of her achieving a 
result as good as the observed one purely by chance.


If her performance was highly unlikely under $H_0$, the null hypothesis 
would be rejected in favor of the alternative, $H_1$, suggesting she 
may truly have the claimed ability. This decision, however, carried the 
risk of a Type~I error---concluding she had the ability when she did 
not. Conversely, failing to reject $H_0$ could result in a Type~II 
error---overlooking a genuine ability due to insufficient evidence. The 
experiment highlighted how statistical decisions are made under 
uncertainty and how error probabilities---Type~I and Type~II---must be 
considered in the design and interpretation of hypothesis tests.
\end{example}


\subsection{Test Statistic and P-Value}

In hypothesis testing, a \emph{test statistic}\index{\emph{test statistic}} 
is a numerical summary of the data used to evaluate the null hypothesis, 
$H_0$. It captures the relevant aspects of the observed data under 
$H_0$ and quantifies the evidence against it. The choice of test 
statistic depends on the specific hypothesis and the structure of the 
data. A test rejects $H_0$ if its test statistic, denoted by $T$, 
falls into a designated \emph{rejection region}\index{\emph{rejection region}}. 
The probability that $T$ falls in this region when $H_0$ is true is 
called the \emph{significance level}, typically denoted by $\alpha$. 
This threshold is usually chosen to be small, such as 0.05 or 0.01, 
to limit the chance of making a Type~I error.


The \emph{p-value} provides an alternative way to summarize results. 
It is defined as the probability, under $H_0$, of obtaining a test 
statistic as extreme as or more extreme than the observed one. A small 
p-value indicates that the observed result would be rare under $H_0$, 
thus providing evidence against it. The decision rule is simple: 
if the p-value is less than $\alpha$, we reject $H_0$; otherwise, we 
fail to reject it. This offers flexibility, because the same p-value
can be compared with different significance levels depending on the
situation.


The p-value is often misunderstood as the probability that $H_0$ is 
true given the data. This is incorrect. The p-value assumes that 
$H_0$ is true and measures how surprising the observed data are under 
that assumption. As shown in empirical studies, this misinterpretation 
is widespread, even among statistically trained researchers 
\citep{falk1995significance}. Understanding that the p-value reflects 
compatibility between the data and the null model---not the truth of 
the hypothesis---is essential for proper statistical reasoning.

Here is a useful check: if a p-value is 0.03, it does not mean there
is a 3\% chance that $H_0$ is true. It means that results at least this
extreme would be uncommon if $H_0$ were true.


\begin{example}[Lady tasting tea (continued)]

In the Lady Tasting Tea experiment, the test statistic $T$ is the 
number of cups the lady correctly identifies as having been prepared 
with milk poured first. The total number of cups is an even number $n$, 
with exactly $n/2$ prepared milk-first and $n/2$ tea-first. Importantly, 
the lady is told in advance that there are exactly $n/2$ of each type, 
and she is required to label exactly $n/2$ cups as milk-first. Under the 
null hypothesis $H_0$, she is assumed to be guessing. In this setup, 
it can be shown that the distribution of $T$ under $H_0$ follows a 
\emph{hypergeometric distribution}\index{distribution!\emph{hypergeometric}}, 
and large values of $T$ provide stronger evidence against $H_0$.


To carry out the test, we fix a significance level $\alpha$, such as
$0.05$, representing the maximum tolerable probability of a Type~I
error. We then define the \emph{rejection region}, consisting of
values of $T$ that are considered too extreme to plausibly occur under
$H_0$. Suppose we choose $\{n/2\}$ as the rejection region—that is, we
only reject $H_0$ if the lady achieves a perfect classification. The
probability of this outcome under $H_0$ is
$\Pr(T = n/2) = 1 / \binom{n}{n/2}$.


For example, suppose $n = 8$ and the lady correctly identifies all 
$4$ milk-first cups. The observed test statistic is 
$T_{\text{obs}} = 4$, and the p-value is 
\[
  \Pr(T \ge T_{\text{obs}} \mid H_0) = 1 / \binom{8}{4}
  = 1/70 \approx  0.0143.
\]
Since this p-value is smaller than the commonly used significance
level $\alpha = 0.05$, we would reject $H_0$ and conclude that her
performance is unlikely to be due to guessing.


Now increase the number of cups to $n = 10$, with the lady again 
achieving a perfect identification of the $5$ milk-first cups. The 
p-value is
\[
  \Pr(T \ge 5 \mid H_0) = 1 / \binom{10}{5}
  = 1 / 252 \approx 0.0040.
\]
This smaller p-value gives even stronger evidence
against~$H_0$---suggesting that her performance is highly unlikely to
be the result of chance guessing and that she may indeed have the 
ability to tell the difference.
\end{example}

\section{Permutation Tests}

The Lady Tasting Tea example belongs to a general family of tests known
as \emph{permutation tests}\index{test!permutation}. A permutation test
asks a simple question: if the labels in the data were shuffled at
random, how unusual would our observed result look?

\subsection{Permutation under Exchangeability}

A permutation test relies on \emph{exchangeability}. This means that
under the null hypothesis, the labels attached to the data could have
been swapped without changing the story. If treatment and control
labels are exchangeable under $H_0$, then any difference between the
groups is treated as random variation rather than a real treatment
effect.

Once the labels are exchangeable, we can shuffle them many times. For
each shuffled dataset, we calculate the same test statistic that we
calculated from the real data. This gives us a picture of what the test
statistic would look like if $H_0$ were true.


By pooling all the data and randomly reassigning the group labels, we 
simulate what the data might look like if $H_0$ were true. Each 
permutation represents a potential arrangement of the data
under~$H_0$, and the distribution of the test statistic across these
permutations forms the \emph{sampling distribution under
  $H_0$}\index{sampling distribution!under $H_0$}. Comparing the
observed test statistic to this distribution allows us to calculate its
p-value, which quantifies how extreme the observed result is
under~$H_0$.


For instance, in the Lady Tasting Tea experiment, the null hypothesis
$H_0$ says that the lady is guessing. Under this assumption, all
possible arrangements of the cups are equally likely. Shuffling the
labels gives an exact picture of chance guessing.

Similarly, suppose we compare the mean outcome in a treatment group
with the mean outcome in a placebo group. If the treatment has no
effect, then the treatment and placebo labels are exchangeable. The
distribution of the shuffled differences in means shows what kinds of
differences we could see by chance.


Sometimes the total number of possible shuffles is too large to list.
In that case, we use a random sample of shuffles.


\subsection{Monte Carlo Approximation}

When it is not practical to use every possible permutation, we can use
a \emph{Monte Carlo approximation}\index{Monte Carlo approximation}.
That means we draw many random permutations and use them to approximate
the sampling distribution under $H_0$.

Permutation tests rely on minimal assumptions, so they are useful when
we do not want to assume a particular population distribution. A small
p-value means that the observed statistic is unusual among the shuffled
versions of the data. That gives evidence against $H_0$.

The key idea is not the shuffling itself, but what the shuffling
represents. Under $H_0$, the labels are supposed to be arbitrary. If
that is true, then the shuffled datasets are reasonable versions of the
world where the null hypothesis holds. The observed dataset is then
compared with this collection of null-world datasets. If the observed
test statistic is near the edge of that collection, the data are
telling us that the null explanation may not be enough.


\section{Properties of Hypothesis Tests}

\subsection{Size and Power}

Hypothesis tests are often judged by two properties: size and power.
Size controls false positives. Power measures how well the test detects
real effects.

The \emph{size} of a hypothesis test is the probability of making a
Type~I error. It is usually set equal to the significance level
$\alpha$. For instance, if $\alpha = 0.05$, then a valid test should
falsely reject $H_0$ about 5\% of the time when $H_0$ is true.

The \emph{power} of a test is the probability of correctly rejecting
$H_0$ when there is a real effect. Power is $1 - \beta$, where $\beta$
is the probability of a Type~II error. Larger samples, larger true
effects, and less variable data usually lead to higher power.

When two tests have the same size, the test with higher power is
usually preferred. But there is a trade-off. Reducing $\alpha$ lowers
the chance of a false positive, but it can also make real effects
harder to detect. Increasing the sample size is often the best way to
gain power without raising the false positive rate.

Here is another way to remember the difference. Size describes how the
test behaves when there is nothing to find. If $H_0$ is true and
$\alpha = 0.05$, a valid test should not cry wolf too often. Power
describes how the test behaves when there is something to find. A test
with low power may miss a real effect, especially when the sample is
small or the data are noisy.

This is why designing a study matters before the data are collected.
If the sample size is too small, even a real effect may be hard to
detect. If the significance level is too strict, false positives become
less likely, but the test may also miss more real effects. Good testing
is therefore not only about choosing a formula. It is also about
understanding the errors we are willing to risk.

In the next example, we will illustrate these 
concepts by comparing two common tests for comparing two 
samples: the t-test and the Wilcoxon test. Using simulations, 
we will examine their size and power under different 
conditions and demonstrate how their performance varies.

\section{Multiple Testing}

When we test one idea, we usually know what a ``significant result''
means. But what happens if we test many ideas at once? Does a small
p-value still carry the same meaning? Let us find out by running our own
simulations.

\begin{example}[20 Ineffective Drugs]
We begin with a simple story: a laboratory screens 20 new drugs to
see whether any of them improves a health score compared with a
placebo. Unknown to us, none of the drugs actually works. By
simulating this world, we can watch what happens when we run many
independent tests in parallel.

returning 20 p-values from 20 two-sample t-tests.

\subsection{The Smallest P-Value}


Every experiment has a ``best'' drug, the one with the smallest p-value.
Let us look at that distribution.

\showChunk{R}{Code/hypo-multiple.R}{minp}

Figure~\ref{fig:ch5:minp} shows that tiny p-values (like 0.01 or
0.005) appear quite often---even though nothing is real. This does not
mean the minimum p-value is useless; indeed, there is a long history of
using extreme values such as the smallest p-value as a valid test
statistic, provided we take into account how many tests were performed.

\begin{figure}[H]
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{images/chapter_5/hypo-minp.pdf}
\caption{Smallest p-value from each 20-drug experiment under pure noise}
\label{fig:ch5:minp}
\end{figure}


The problem arises when we look at many tests, take the smallest
p-value, and then interpret it as if it came from a single test. In
that naive view, we are ignoring the fact that we gave ourselves many
chances to see something ``exciting.''


\subsection{When Some Effects Are Real}

Now let us simulate a world where 4 out of 20 drugs truly have an
effect.

\showChunk{R}{Code/hypo-multiple.R}{do1screen_alt}

We compare two strategies:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Uncorrected testing: $p < 0.05$
\item Bonferroni correction: $p < 0.05/20$
\end{enumerate}

We simulate many experiments and count true positives (TP) and false
positives (FP).

\showChunk{R}{Code/hypo-multiple.R}{altresults}

Uncorrected tests find many real effects but also many fake ones.
Bonferroni greatly reduces false positives, but also misses some real
drugs. The simulation reveals a simple trade-off:

Reducing false discoveries often reduces real discoveries too.



From these experiments, we discovered:
\begin{itemize}
\item A p-value only behaves as expected when we test one idea.
\item When we test many ideas, small p-values appear even if nothing is
  true.
\item The question ``What is the chance that at least one test shows
  significance?'' becomes central.
\item Adjustments like Bonferroni reduce false discoveries but make true
  effects harder to detect.
\item Simulation shows all of this clearly---no formulas required.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary}
  
In this chapter we introduced the logic of hypothesis testing and
showed how randomness can lead to surprising results if we do not
analyze data carefully. We learned how to state a null hypothesis,
simulate what data might look like under this null, and compare
observed outcomes to what would be expected by chance. Through several
examples, we saw how simulation helps us quantify uncertainty and
evaluate whether an observed effect is genuinely unusual. In the next
chapter, we shift our attention to estimation---how to infer unknown
quantities from data and measure how accurate these estimates are.

\begin{exercise}
Suppose you flip a coin 20 times and observe 16 heads. Build a
simulation in R where the coin is fair, using \lstinline{rbinom()} or
\lstinline{sample()}. Estimate the probability of seeing 16 or more

\begin{exercise}
Repeat the pure-noise experiment with $m = 100$
  independent t-tests per experiment. What is the probability that at

  $m = 20$. What do you notice?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Modify the simulation with four true effects by changing
  the effect size to 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0. How do these affect true and
  false discoveries under the uncorrected and Bonferroni approaches?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
In the pure-noise setting, record the largest
  t-statistic (in absolute value) from each experiment. Plot its
  distribution. Why does the largest value tend to be quite large even
  when nothing is happening?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Create your own ``multiple testing world.'' You may use
  many regressions, many correlations, or many group comparisons.
  Simulate data, run all tests, and study the distribution of the
  discovered ``findings.'' What is the biggest surprise you observe?
\end{exercise}
